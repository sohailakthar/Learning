11th President of India

Overview

Avul Pakir Jainulabdeen Abdul Kalam BR (15 October 1931 – 27 July 2015) was the 11th President of India (2002-2007), renowned as an aerospace scientist. Born in Rameswaram, Tamil Nadu, he excelled in physics and aerospace engineering. His extensive career spanned the Defence Research and Development Organisation (DRDO) and Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO), earning him the title "Missile Man of India" for his pioneering work in missile technology and space programs. He played a key role in India's nuclear tests in 1998. Kalam's presidency was marked by widespread popularity, and after his term, he continued his pursuits in education, writing, and public service. He received the Bharat Ratna, India's highest civilian award. Kalam tragically passed away due to cardiac arrest while delivering a lecture in Shillong in 2015. His funeral in Rameswaram drew thousands of mourners and dignitaries, honoring his legacy.

Early Life and Education

Avul Pakir Jainulabdeen Abdul Kalam, born on 15 October 1931 in Rameswaram, Tamil Nadu, hailed from a Tamil Muslim family. His father, Jainulabdeen Marakayar, was a mosque imam and boat owner, while his mother, Ashiamma, was a housewife. As the youngest of five siblings, Kalam's family had a history of trade and land ownership but faced financial struggles. Their business, involving ferrying pilgrims and trading, declined with the opening of the Pamban Bridge in 1914. Despite their impoverished circumstances, Kalam's determination to learn was evident from a young age. He sold newspapers to contribute to the family income.

Kalam displayed dedication to his studies, particularly in Mathematics. After completing his education at Schwartz Higher Secondary School and Saint Joseph's College, Tiruchirappalli, he graduated in physics from the University of Madras in 1954. He pursued aerospace engineering at Madras Institute of Technology. Notably, Kalam overcame a challenging project deadline set by the Dean, impressing him. Although he narrowly missed his aspiration of becoming a fighter pilot due to ranking ninth in qualifiers, his journey toward becoming a renowned scientist and statesman was well underway.

Career as a Scientist

After completing his studies at the Madras Institute of Technology in 1960, Abdul Kalam joined the Aeronautical Development Establishment of the Defence Research and Development Organisation (DRDO) as a scientist, marking the beginning of his illustrious career. His initial work included designing a small hovercraft, but he soon transitioned to the Indian National Committee for Space Research (INCOSPAR), later known as ISRO. Under the guidance of renowned space scientist Vikram Sarabhai, Kalam's contributions to India's space endeavors became significant.

In 1969, Kalam took on a pivotal role at the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO), leading the development of India's first Satellite Launch Vehicle (SLV-III) that successfully deployed the Rohini satellite in 1980. His involvement with expandable rocket projects dates back to his time at DRDO in 1965. Kalam's expertise extended to the development of the Polar Satellite Launch Vehicle (PSLV) and SLV-III projects, both of which achieved notable success in the 1970s and 1990s.

Kalam's influence also extended to the defense sector, where he played a crucial part in projects such as Project Devil and Project Valiant, aimed at developing ballistic missiles based on SLV technology. His leadership in research and education earned him prestige, leading to the initiation of the Integrated Guided Missile Development Programme (IGMDP). This program encompassed the development of missiles like Agni and Prithvi, although some projects faced criticism for management and budget issues.

From 1992 to 1999, Kalam served as the Chief Scientific Adviser to the Prime Minister and Secretary of the Defence Research and Development Organisation. He played an intensive role in the Pokhran-II nuclear tests and became a prominent figure in India's nuclear science landscape. Kalam's contributions extended beyond science; he collaborated with cardiologist Soma Raju to develop a low-cost coronary stent and later designed a tablet computer for healthcare in rural areas.

Presidency

Avul Pakir Jainulabdeen Abdul Kalam, the 11th President of India, served from 25 July 2002 to 25 July 2007. His election was supported by the National Democratic Alliance (NDA) and backed by the Samajwadi Party and the Nationalist Congress Party. Kalam filed his nomination in June 2002, and the polling took place on 18 July, resulting in his easy victory. During his presidency, he was known as the "People's President" and was praised for his decision-making, although he faced criticism for his handling of mercy petitions and the imposition of President's Rule in Bihar in 2005.

Kalam's tenure saw his advocacy for a Uniform Civil Code and his support for various political fronts. He was the third President of India to receive the Bharat Ratna and the first scientist to occupy Rashtrapati Bhavan. Kalam considered a second term but later declined, expressing gratitude for the public's support.

In 2012, there were discussions about nominating Kalam for a second term, although he eventually declined. Throughout his presidency, Kalam remained a beloved figure, appreciating the support and trust of the people.

Post-presidency

After concluding his presidential term, Kalam held various academic positions, contributing to several prestigious institutions. He became a visiting professor at the Indian Institute of Management Shillong, Ahmedabad, and Indore. He also received an honorary fellowship from the Indian Institute of Science, Bangalore, and served as the chancellor of the Indian Institute of Space Science and Technology Thiruvananthapuram.

Kalam's academic engagements extended to his role as a professor of Aerospace Engineering at Anna University, where he also taught information technology at the International Institute of Information Technology, Hyderabad, and technology at Banaras Hindu University and Anna University.

In 2011, Kalam's support for the Koodankulam Nuclear Power Plant sparked criticism from civil groups who accused him of not addressing the concerns of local residents. Despite his assurances on safety features, some viewed him as a pro-nuclear scientist and questioned his stance.

In May 2012, Kalam initiated the "What Can I Give Movement," a program aimed at Indian youth, focusing on combating corruption and promoting positive change.

Religious and spiritual views

Daily namaz (prayer) and fasting during Ramadan were important practices in Kalam's life as a devout Sunni Muslim. His father, an imam, instilled these Islamic customs in him and his siblings. Kalam emphasized interfaith respect and dialogue, influenced by discussions his father had with a Hindu priest and a church priest. He believed in the value of cooperation among religious, social, and political leaders to address India's challenges. Kalam considered religion a means of building friendships and advocated for respect for other faiths, often saying, "For great men, religion is a way of making friends; small people make religion a fighting tool."

Death

On 27 July 2015, Kalam delivered a lecture in Shillong but experienced discomfort and collapsed shortly after beginning. He was rushed to Bethany Hospital but was declared dead due to a sudden cardiac arrest. His last words to his aide were, "Funny guy! Are you doing well?"

After his death, his body was flown to Delhi, where it was received by dignitaries and the public. It was then taken to his residence at 10 Rajaji Marg for homage. On 29 July, his body was flown to Madurai and then to Rameswaram, his hometown. A public display allowed people to pay their respects. On 30 July 2015, Kalam was laid to rest at Rameswaram's Pei Karumbu Ground with full state honors, attended by a massive gathering including political leaders and dignitaries.
