Overview

Sarojini Naidu (1879–1949) was an Indian political activist and poet, known as the "Nightingale of India." She held significant roles in the Indian independence movement, advocating civil rights, women's emancipation, and anti-imperialism. Naidu was the first Indian woman to lead the Indian National Congress and serve as a state governor. Born in Hyderabad to a Bengali family, she studied in Madras, London, and Cambridge, engaging in suffrage work in Britain. Inspired by Mahatma Gandhi's principles of swaraj, she joined the national movement, eventually becoming Congress president in 1925. Her poetic work, praised for its imagery and lyrical quality, covered various themes, including patriotism and tragedy. Notably, her poem "In the Bazaars of Hyderabad," published in 1912, remains widely celebrated.

Personal Life

Sarojini Naidu, born on 13 February 1879 in Hyderabad, was the eldest child of Aghorenath Chattopadhyay, a Bengali Brahmin and the principal of Nizam College. Her father, who hailed from Brahmangaon Bikrampur in Dhaka, held a doctorate of Science from Edinburgh University. Her mother was a Bengali poet. Sarojini had seven siblings, including Virendranath Chattopadhyay, a revolutionary, and Harindranath Chattopadhyay, a poet and dramatist. Their family, known for its contributions, enjoyed a respected status in Hyderabad.

Education

Sarojini Naidu excelled in her matriculation examination, securing the highest rank, at the age of twelve in 1891. She pursued further studies in England from 1895 to 1898, attending King's College, London, and Girton College, Cambridge, where she received a scholarship sponsored by the Nizam of Hyderabad. During her time in England, she encountered artists associated with the Aesthetic and Decadent movements.

Marriage

After returning to Hyderabad in 1898, Chattopadhyay married Govindaraju Naidu, a physician she had met during her time in England. Their marriage, deemed "groundbreaking and scandalous," received approval from both families. The union was harmonious, and they had five children. Their daughter Padmaja actively participated in the Quit India Movement and later held significant government positions in independent India.

Political Career

Starting in 1904, Naidu gained popularity as an eloquent speaker, advocating for Indian independence and women's rights, particularly women's education. Her speeches often followed the Nyaya rhetorical structure. Notable addresses included the Indian National Congress and Indian Social Conference in 1906. Naidu's social efforts during a flood earned her the Kaisar-i-Hind Medal in 1911, which she later returned in protest of the Jallianwala Bagh massacre. She met Mahatma Gandhi in 1914, inspiring her political commitment. Naidu became the second woman President of the Indian National Congress and, alongside Muthulakshmi Reddy, founded the Women's Indian Association in 1917. She supported universal suffrage in London with Annie Besant and the Lucknow Pact in India. Close to Gandhi, Naidu joined his satyagraha movement in 1917 and participated in the Salt March. She led the campaign after Gandhi's arrest and took part in Round Table Conferences. Jailed by the British in 1932 and 1942, she became India's first female governor in 1947, serving until her death in 1949.

Writing Career

Sarojini Naidu began writing at 12, impressing the Nizam of Hyderabad with her play "Maher Muneer" in Persian. Her English poetry, influenced by British Romanticism, showcased rich sensory imagery and vivid depictions of India. She was often referred to as the "Indian Yeats." Her first book, "The Golden Threshold," published in 1905, introduced her lyrical style. "The Bird of Time," published in 1912, displayed strong nationalist themes, including the famous poem "In the Bazaars of Hyderabad." "The Broken Wing" (1917) and "The Gift of India" criticized British exploitation. Her speeches were collected in "The Speeches and Writings of Sarojini Naidu." She edited works, including "Muhammad Ali Jinnah, An Ambassador of Unity," and her complete poems were published posthumously in "The Feather of the Dawn" (1961).

Death

Sarojini Naidu passed away due to cardiac arrest at 3:30 p.m. (IST) on March 2, 1949, at the Government House in Lucknow. Her health had deteriorated after returning from New Delhi on February 15, leading to the cancellation of official engagements. Bloodletting was performed on March 1 after she complained of a severe headache, and she collapsed following a fit of cough. Naidu's last moments included her requesting the attending nurse to sing to her, after which she peacefully slept. Her final rites took place at the Gomati River.
